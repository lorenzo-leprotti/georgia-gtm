## Q1 — Paper Mill Maintenance Spend in Georgia

### Active Georgia Kraft Pulp and Tissue Mills (post-2025 closures)

Georgia had 11 operational pulp and paper mills as of early 2025. After three closures — GP Cedar Springs (August 2025) and International Paper's Savannah and Riceboro mills (September 2025) — the count dropped to **8 active mills**, down from a peak of 18 in 1977.

Sources: Capitol Beat (August 2025), The Current GA (September 2025), Forisk Consulting Mill Database (August 2025).

**Currently active kraft/cellulose/tissue mills in Georgia:**
- GP Brunswick Cellulose (kraft/fluff pulp, 951,000 t/yr)
- GP Savannah River / Rincon (tissue)
- Clearwater Paper Augusta (SBS paperboard)
- Five others (smaller converting/specialty facilities)

### Annual Shutdown Maintenance Cost at a Large Kraft/Pulp Mill

**Clearwater Paper Augusta — primary data (most authoritative Georgia-specific figure):**
- Q1 2025 earnings call (Motley Fool transcript, April 2026): **$15–17M total direct outage cost per annual shutdown** at Augusta
- Q3 2025 earnings (Clearwater Paper IR, October 2025): actual outturn **~$16M** for the Augusta major maintenance outage
- Clearwater Paper company-wide: $70–80M in annual total maintenance spend; $45–50M in major outage direct costs across *three mills* (Augusta + Lewiston ID + Cypress Bend AR) in 2025 — per Q4 2025 earnings (Yahoo Finance / Clearwater Paper IR, February 2026)

> ⚠️ **Critical correction for the GTM document:** The "$50M at one mid-sized paper mill" framing is wrong. The $50M covers all three Clearwater mills combined. Augusta alone = ~$16M in total direct outage cost, implying **~$3.2M in scaffolding labor** at Augusta (at the 20% scaffold share). This must be corrected before the document is shown externally.

**Industry benchmarks (no Georgia-specific primary source beyond Clearwater):**
- Pulp and Paper Canada (May 2017): A kraft pulp mill annual 10–14 day shutdown costs "millions of dollars" and represents a material quarterly cash flow impact. No $/shutdown figure published.
- ProcessBarron (engineering firm, August 2021): "A standard annual five-to-ten-day shutdown for a paper mill typically takes up a third of their maintenance budget." At Augusta's $70–80M total maintenance envelope, this implies ~$23–27M per shutdown — bracketing the confirmed $16M.
- TAPPI *Paper360°* (April 2022): Notes that maintenance cost/ton and maintenance cost/ERV are the dominant industry benchmarks; cautions against point estimates without mill-specific context. No public $/shutdown figure published.

## Strategy Assessment: Does This Work?

### What Works Well

**Geographic concentration is the document's strongest argument.** Having two of the largest asset owners (Georgia Power, Georgia-Pacific) and the dominant scaffold contractor (BrandSafway) all headquartered within 30 miles of each other in metro Atlanta is genuinely unusual. Most industrial geographies force parallel owner and contractor conversations across multiple travel days; in Atlanta, both can happen in the same week. This is a real and defensible sales efficiency advantage.

**The recurring-revenue logic at the power plants is solid.** Plant Vogtle's four-unit staggered schedule creates a near-continuous outage calendar. Plant Bowen's all-four-units-active-through-2038 profile (per Georgia Power's 2025 IRP) gives KEWAZO a multi-year window. These are annuity-like maintenance cycles, not one-and-done construction projects.

**The buyer model (owner approval → contractor deployment) is structurally correct.** This is how industrial technology adoption works in process industries. The document accurately identifies the two-step sales motion required.

### Risk 1: Channel Tension Is Harder Than It Looks

The document acknowledges BrandSafway's channel tension in one paragraph and largely dismisses it via "labor shortage buffer" and "owner mandates." In practice, this is harder. BrandSafway generates ~$5B/year by deploying labor. A technology that credibly reduces scaffolding labor hours by 40–70% represents a potential revenue reduction — not just a margin threat.

The "owner mandate" mechanism requires asset owners to write LIFTBOT into the TAR specification 12–18 months before the event, during outage planning. That means KEWAZO's sales team must influence the owner's internal planning process well before the contractor is even engaged — a long, technically complex sales cycle for a small company entering a new market. The document would benefit from acknowledging this and explaining how KEWAZO plans to get into the owner's planning cycle (e.g., a pilot at one Clearwater or GP mill that generates a documented cost result the owner can reference in subsequent TAR specifications).

### Risk 2: Mill Closure Trend Reduces the T1 Count

The closure of three Georgia mills in summer 2025 reduced the pulp-and-paper T1 target list from 6 to 3 active mills. What remains (GP Brunswick, GP Rincon, Clearwater Augusta) is higher-quality — larger, better-capitalized, with confirmed multi-million-dollar outage programs — but the addressable count is smaller than it was 12 months ago. The GTM document does not acknowledge this structural shift, which a sophisticated reader (investor, board member, potential partner) will notice and should be addressed proactively.

### Risk 3: The $350M Aggregate Needs the Math

A CEO or investor will ask "how did you get to $350M?" A defensible bottom-up build using confirmed and sourced figures:

| Site | Annual Direct Outage Cost (est.) | Scaffolding Share (20%) |
|---|---|---|
| Clearwater Paper Augusta | $16M (confirmed, Q3 2025 earnings) | $3.2M |
| GP Brunswick Cellulose | $10–15M (estimated; no public figure) | $2–3M |
| GP Rincon | $8–12M (estimated tissue mill) | $1.6–2.4M |
| Plant Vogtle (4 units, staggered ~18 mo) | ~$20M/unit × 4 ÷ 1.5 = ~$53M/yr equivalent | $10.6M |
| Plant Hatch (2 units, staggered ~18 mo) | ~$18M/unit × 2 ÷ 1.5 = ~$24M/yr equivalent | $4.8M |
| Plant Bowen (4 units, every 3 yr) | ~$15M/unit × 4 ÷ 3 = ~$20M/yr equivalent | $4M |
| Plant Scherer (3 units, every 3 yr) | ~$15M/unit × 3 ÷ 3 = ~$15M/yr equivalent | $3M |
| Plant Wansley (gas CC, every 4 yr) | ~$8M/unit × 4 ÷ 4 = ~$8M/yr equivalent | $1.6M |
| **Total** | **~$164M/yr in direct outage cost** | **~$33M/yr in scaffolding labor** |

This $164M figure is a far more defensible "floor" than $350M, and the $33M in addressable scaffolding labor — with LIFTBOT reducing it by 40–70% — represents **$13M–$23M in annual savings available to Georgia T1 owners**. That is still a substantial, board-level number, and showing the math makes the pitch more credible than an unanchored $350M claim.

### Overall Verdict

The strategy is directionally correct and commercially logical. The target set is real, the buyer model is accurate, and the Atlanta geography creates a genuine sales efficiency advantage that is rare in industrial markets. The risks are execution-side — long sales cycle, channel tension with BrandSafway, nuclear site access requirements — rather than market-side. Fix the four factual corrections, add the bottom-up sizing math, and acknowledge the mill closure trend explicitly. The result is a materially stronger document that holds up to expert scrutiny.
